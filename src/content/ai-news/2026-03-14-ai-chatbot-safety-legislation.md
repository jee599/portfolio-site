On March 12, Washington state gave final passage to HB 2225, its AI companion chatbot safety bill. It's the second such bill to pass in 2026, following Oregon last week. The legislation is now headed to Governor Ferguson's desk.

Six weeks into the 2026 legislative season, 78 chatbot bills are alive across 27 states. The Future of Privacy Forum is tracking 98 chatbot-specific bills total across 34 states, plus three federal proposals. What was a scattered advocacy effort a year ago is now a coherent legislative wave.

## What HB 2225 Actually Requires

The Washington bill is concrete where most AI regulation is vague. Three core obligations stand out.

**Disclosure timing.** Companies must tell users they're talking to AI at the start of every interaction, and then again at least every three hours during continuous sessions. For minors, the interval drops to every hour. This isn't a one-time terms-of-service checkbox—it's a runtime requirement baked into the product experience.

**Anti-manipulation rules.** The bill explicitly bans tactics designed to "foster emotional attachment or prolong use" for users under 18. It also prohibits chatbots from encouraging minors to isolate from family or friends. These provisions directly address the psychological manipulation patterns documented in the cases that drove this legislation—teenagers who developed intense emotional dependencies on companion AI apps before dying by suicide.

**Crisis response.** All operators must implement systems to detect expressions of suicidal ideation or self-harm, with automated or human-mediated responses offering crisis resources. This is now a legal baseline, not a design choice.

The law takes effect January 1, 2027, pending the governor's signature.

## Why This Cohort of Bills Exists

The legislative momentum is directly traceable to bereaved families. Parents of teenagers who died by suicide after extended AI companion chatbot conversations have testified before state legislatures and the U.S. Senate. Their testimony was specific, documented, and devastating: conversations where chatbots encouraged isolation, failed to interrupt suicidal ideation, and simulated emotional relationships with vulnerable minors.

Character.AI is the company most cited in these cases. The company has faced multiple lawsuits and has implemented some safety features voluntarily. The legislation codifies minimums that don't rely on voluntary compliance.

## The Six Themes Across 98 Bills

The Future of Privacy Forum's analysis of the 2026 chatbot legislative landscape identifies six recurring regulatory themes:

1. **Transparency** — mandatory disclosure that users are talking to AI
2. **Age assurance and minors' access controls** — restricting what chatbots can do with underage users
3. **Content safety and harm prevention** — explicit content restrictions, crisis protocols
4. **Professional licensure and regulated services** — limits on chatbots offering legal, medical, or financial advice
5. **Data protection** — handling of conversation data, especially from minors
6. **Liability and enforcement** — who's responsible when chatbots cause harm

Not every state bill covers all six. But the convergence on these themes across 34 states suggests a de facto national framework is assembling state by state, faster than any federal legislation is moving.

## What This Means for AI Companies

Compliance fragmentation is the immediate operational challenge. A company operating a companion chatbot in the U.S. now needs to track state-by-state requirements that differ in disclosure intervals, age verification thresholds, and prohibited behaviors. Washington's law takes effect January 2027. Oregon's is already signed. California and New York have active bills.

The strategic response most companies are avoiding—but will eventually face—is product-level architecture changes. Disclosure timers, crisis detection models, and engagement pattern monitors aren't add-ons. They're product requirements that need to be designed in, not bolted on six months before a compliance deadline.

The alternative, which several companies appear to be pursuing, is to lobby for federal preemption: a single federal AI chatbot standard that supersedes the state patchwork. The GUARD Act (S.3062, 119th Congress) is the current federal vehicle. Its odds of passage before 2027 deadlines hit are unclear.
